Getting started.

From "I just downloaded the installer" to "my printer boots into Mainsail." Most printers need about 15 minutes of your actual attention. The rest is the printer doing things by itself while you make coffee.

Before you start

If you have a UPS, plug the printer into it. Path B writes the eMMC for ~20 minutes, and a power cut mid-write is the one way you can actually brick this thing. Path A doesn't have that risk, the snapshot rolls you back if anything goes wrong.

What you'll need

  • A Klipper-based printer (profiles list) on the same network as your laptop.
  • The printer's IP or mDNS hostname. Deckhand will try to find it, but knowing it speeds things up.
  • SSH login for the printer's admin account. Vendor defaults are listed below.
  • About 2 GB free on your laptop for cached firmware images.
  • A normal home network. mDNS makes life easier but isn't required.

1. Install Deckhand

Grab the installer for your OS from releases. The builds are signed.

Windows
Deckhand-0.4.1-win-x64.exe
SmartScreen will probably warn for new builds. Click "More info" then "Run anyway."
macOS
Deckhand-0.4.1-macos.dmg
Universal binary. Apple Silicon and Intel both work natively.
Linux
Deckhand-0.4.1-linux-x86_64.AppImage
Needs pkexec on PATH for the elevated helper.

2. First launch: the Doctor preflight

Screen S10 runs a bunch of self-checks before letting you near a flash. Greens are fine. Warns are usually fine. Reds need to be sorted before you continue.

Preflight report doctor.run
✓ runtime , darwin/arm64, sidecar 0.4.1
✓ elevated_helper , next to sidecar
✓ disks_enumerate , 3 devices
✓ data_dir , ~/Library/Application Support/Deckhand
✓ macos_on_path , osascript found
⚠ mdns_resolvable , UDP/5353 blocked, auto-discovery disabled
✓ github_rate_limit , 58/60 remaining
✓ clock_skew , 0.4s

That amber row means your firewall is dropping mDNS. No big deal, just type the printer's IP manually on the next screen. Hard fails (no pkexec on Linux, can't write to the data dir) gate the flash screens but you can keep poking around the rest of the app.

4. Pick a path

S40 asks the big question: keep the stock OS or wipe it? "Two ways through it" walks through the trade-offs if you're unsure. If your printer works fine and you just want the stack swapped, go with Stock keep.

5. Walk the wizard

What comes next depends on your path. The right rail shows the full step list. You can hit Back any time, answers stick, and the wizard rearranges itself if you change your firmware or web UI choice.

  1. Snapshot. Tick what you want preserved (default: everything). Deckhand tarballs it to your laptop, in ~/.deckhand/snapshots/.
  2. Firmware. Kalico or Klipper.
  3. Web UI. Mainsail, Fluidd, both, or neither.
  4. KIAUH. Yes, install it. You'll want it later for updates.
  5. Screen daemon. If your printer has a touchscreen, pick the daemon. "None" is also a fine answer.
  6. Per-service decisions. "Disable makerbase-net-mods?" "Remove the vendor splash screen?" One question per screen, you can mostly hit yes.
  7. Cleanup files. Tick which leftover files to delete.
  8. Hardening. Optional but worth it. Change the SSH password, fix the clock, turn off the random UDP listeners the vendor left running.

6. Hit Run

S900 streams every step's output. The right rail shows step status; click any step to see the commands it ran. If a step fails the run pauses, you get Retry / Skip / Cancel. If your laptop goes to sleep mid-run, the printer remembers where it was, just open the app again and click Resume.

When it's done

S910 shows everything that changed and links straight to your new web UI (usually http://printer.local). The snapshot stays on your laptop in case you want anything back later.
